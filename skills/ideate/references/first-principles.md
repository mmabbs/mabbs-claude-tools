# First Principles ("Axiomatic Thinking")

**Core operation:** Decompose a problem to its fundamental truths — things that are true regardless of convention — then rebuild your solution from scratch using only those truths.

## Why It Works

Most thinking is reasoning by analogy: "This is like that, so we should do what they did." Analogical reasoning is fast but inherits all the assumptions and constraints of the original solution. First principles thinking strips those away and asks: what must be true?

## Process

1. **Name the problem** — What are you solving?
2. **List current assumptions** — How is this currently approached? Why?
3. **Strip to fundamentals** — What is *physically, logically, or mathematically* true, independent of convention?
4. **Rebuild from those truths** — What solution would you design if nothing existed yet?
5. **Compare** — How does your rebuilt solution differ from the conventional one? Why?

## The Decomposition Test

For each belief in your plan, ask:
- **Is this a fundamental truth?** (Physics, math, human nature, economics)
- **Or is it inherited convention?** (Industry standard, "best practice", how it's always been done)

If it's convention, it's negotiable.

## Applications

| Context | Fundamental Question |
|---------|---------------------|
| **Pricing** | What does this actually cost to deliver? (Ignore competitor pricing) |
| **Process** | What steps are physically necessary? (Ignore inherited workflow) |
| **Product** | What does the user fundamentally need? (Ignore existing feature set) |
| **Career** | What do I actually need to live well? (Ignore status conventions) |
| **Learning** | What must I understand to derive the rest? (Ignore curriculum order) |

## Red Flags You Need This

- "Everyone does it this way" — but nobody can explain why
- Iterating on an inherited solution that feels fundamentally wrong
- The cost structure or timeline "just is what it is"
- You're benchmarking against competitors instead of against the problem itself
- "Best practice" is being used as an argument-ender

## First Principles Prompts

- "What are the raw inputs and desired outputs? Forget the middle."
- "If we were starting from zero, would we build it this way?"
- "What would this cost if we manufactured the constraints ourselves?"
- "Which of these steps exist because of physics, and which because of habit?"

## Distinction from `assume`

`assume` (Assumption Archaeology) surfaces hidden beliefs so you can question them. `axiom` goes further — it **discards the entire inherited frame** and reconstructs from base truths. `assume` is "what am I taking for granted?" `axiom` is "forget everything — what must be true?"

## Remember

- First principles thinking is expensive — don't use it for every decision
- Best applied to high-stakes problems where conventional solutions feel inadequate
- The rebuild doesn't have to be radical — sometimes you rediscover the conventional solution and now understand *why* it's right
- Pair with `constrain` to test your rebuilt solution against real-world constraints
