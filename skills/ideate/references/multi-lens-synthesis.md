# Multi-Lens Synthesis ("Perspective Aggregation")

**Core operation:** Combine multiple viewpoints into unified understanding while *preserving* divergence rather than resolving it prematurely.

## Why It Works

Traditional synthesis flattens disagreement into consensus. This preserves the information content of disagreement—where perspectives diverge tells you something important.

## Lens Construction

Before synthesizing, you need lenses worth synthesizing. Don't default to generic roles ("the optimist," "the pragmatist") — construct perspectives that create real tension for *this specific problem*.

### How Many

- **3–5 lenses.** Fewer than 3 collapses into binary thinking. More than 5 rarely adds signal proportional to the complexity it introduces.
- **Let the problem decide.** If the problem has 5 natural fault lines, use 5. If it has 3 clear stakeholder groups, use 3. Don't pad to hit a number or trim to stay under one.
- **The test:** Every lens must produce at least one claim the others wouldn't. If two lenses keep agreeing, merge them.

### Selection Dimensions

Vary lenses along at least two of these axes:

| Axis | What it varies | Example poles |
|------|---------------|---------------|
| **Optimization target** | What each lens is trying to maximize | Revenue vs. user trust vs. speed-to-market vs. long-term optionality |
| **Time horizon** | When success is measured | This week vs. this quarter vs. 3 years out |
| **Risk posture** | Tolerance for downside | "Protect what we have" vs. "Bet on upside" |
| **Proximity** | Distance from the problem | Builder (hands-on) vs. User (affected) vs. Observer (pattern-matching from outside) |
| **Methodology** | How they'd gather evidence | Quantitative vs. qualitative vs. structural vs. experiential |

### Specificity Rule

Each lens must be specific enough that it implies a *methodology*, not just an opinion.

| Too generic | Specific enough |
|-------------|-----------------|
| "The skeptic" | "A platform engineer who's been burned by premature abstractions" |
| "The user" | "A first-time user who found this through a Google search, not a referral" |
| "The business person" | "A CFO evaluating this against three competing budget requests" |
| "The creative" | "A narrative designer who builds systems players discover, not ones explained to them" |

The test: **Could two different people interpret this lens the same way?** If the label is so vague that it could produce wildly different perspectives depending on who's imagining it, it's not specific enough.

### Stance Assignment

Not all lenses should be collaborative. Assign stance deliberately:

- **At least one lens should be adversarial** — actively looking for why this fails
- **At least one should be constructive** — building on what works
- **At least one should be orthogonal** — approaching from a direction the problem statement doesn't anticipate

## Process

1. **Construct lenses** — Using the dimensions above, build 3–5 perspectives that create genuine tension for this problem
2. **Generate each perspective independently** — What does each lens see? What would it recommend? What does it worry about?
3. **Map common ground** — What do all lenses agree on? (This is your high-confidence foundation)
4. **Map divergence points** — Where and *why* do they disagree?
5. **Extract meta-insight** — What does the *pattern* of agreement/disagreement reveal about the problem's actual structure?
6. **Quantify remaining uncertainty** — What's still unknown even after synthesis?

## The Meta-Level Question

Instead of "Who is right?" ask:
- **What conditions** would make each perspective correct?
- **What information** does each perspective have that others lack?
- **What values** is each perspective optimizing for?

## Applications

| Context | How to Apply |
|---------|--------------|
| **Decision-making** | Construct lenses around the decision's key tradeoffs → the pattern of disagreement reveals the actual decision factors |
| **User research** | Contradictory feedback → negative space between perspectives reveals the real issue |
| **Team conflict** | Don't pick a winner; map what each position sees that others miss |
| **Self-reflection** | Your past selves had different beliefs—aggregate rather than dismiss |
| **Strategy** | Lenses optimizing for different time horizons reveal which moves are robust across futures |

## Anti-Patterns

| Failure mode | What happens | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Generic labels | "Optimist vs. pessimist" produces nothing you couldn't guess | Apply the specificity rule — each lens implies a methodology |
| Echo chamber | All lenses reach the same conclusion | Vary optimization targets more aggressively; add an orthogonal lens |
| Premature consensus | Rushing to "the answer" before mapping divergence | Linger at step 4 — divergence IS the insight |
| Lens bloat | 7+ perspectives, diminishing returns | Merge lenses that produce identical reasoning; cap at 5 |
| Decoration | Lenses named but not actually used to generate distinct analysis | Each lens must produce at least one claim the others wouldn't make |

## Distinction from `steelman`

`steelman` builds the strongest case for ONE opposing position. `lens` holds MULTIPLE positions simultaneously without advocating for any. Use `steelman` when you need to stress-test a specific decision; use `lens` when you need to understand a problem's full shape.

## Red Flags You Need This

- "Everyone has a different opinion"
- Feeling pressure to pick one perspective as "right"
- Feedback seems contradictory but everyone seems reasonable
- You're dismissing perspectives rather than integrating them
- A decision "feels right" but you can't articulate what you're trading away
