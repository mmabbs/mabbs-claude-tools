# Steelmanning ("Strongest Opposing Case")

**Core operation:** Build the most compelling, charitable, and rigorous version of the argument you disagree with — then see if your position survives.

## Why It Works

Strawmanning (weakening the opposing argument to defeat it easily) feels good but teaches nothing. Steelmanning forces you to genuinely understand why smart people hold the opposing view. If your position survives the strongest counter-argument, it's robust. If it doesn't, you've learned something more valuable than winning.

## Process

1. **State your current position** — What do you believe?
2. **Identify the opposing view** — Who disagrees, and why?
3. **Steelman the opposition** — Build the *strongest possible version* of their argument, including evidence and reasoning you might not have considered
4. **Argue *for* the steelman** — Try to convince yourself. Where is it genuinely right?
5. **Test your position** — Does your original view survive the strongest counter-argument?
6. **Update** — Modify, strengthen, or abandon your position based on what you learned

## The Steelman Test

Your steelman is strong enough when:
- An advocate of that position would say "yes, that's my argument — maybe even better than I'd put it"
- You can identify *genuine* strengths in it, not just acknowledge it politely
- You feel a moment of doubt about your own position — that's the signal it's working

## Applications

| Context | Steelman Question |
|---------|------------------|
| **Product decision** | "Why would a smart person choose the approach I rejected?" |
| **Hiring debate** | "What's the strongest case for the candidate I'm skeptical of?" |
| **Strategy** | "Why might the opposite strategy actually be smarter?" |
| **Feedback** | "What's the most charitable interpretation of this criticism?" |
| **Conflict** | "If I fully adopted their perspective, what would I see?" |
| **Self-doubt** | "What's the strongest case that I *am* on the right track?" |

## Red Flags You Need This

- "They just don't get it" — you haven't understood their argument
- Dismissing opposition without engaging with their strongest points
- Surrounding yourself with agreement — echo chamber risk
- Making a decision by only building the case *for* it
- Feeling certain about a complex, contested question

## Steelman Prompts

- "What's the best version of the argument I disagree with?"
- "Why might a smart, well-intentioned person hold the opposite view?"
- "What evidence would I need to see to change my mind?"
- "If I were arguing the other side, what would my three strongest points be?"
- "What am I dismissing too quickly?"

## Distinction from `lens`

`lens` (Multi-Lens Synthesis) aggregates *multiple* perspectives and synthesizes them without flattening disagreement. `steelman` commits *fully* to **one opposing view** and builds the strongest possible version of it. `lens` is diplomatic — it preserves all perspectives. `steelman` is adversarial (against yourself) — it tries to defeat your own position.

## Anti-patterns

| Trap | What It Looks Like |
|------|-------------------|
| **Weak steelman** | "I guess they have a point, but..." — you haven't genuinely engaged |
| **Performative steelman** | Going through the motions without actually considering you might be wrong |
| **Endless steelmanning** | Using it to avoid making a decision — at some point, commit |

## Remember

- The goal isn't to change your mind — it's to earn your confidence by stress-testing your position
- If you can't steelman the opposition, you don't understand the debate well enough to have an opinion
- Sometimes the steelman is so strong that you *should* update your view — that's a feature, not a failure
- Pair with `lens` when there are more than two sides — steelman each one
- Pair with `assume` to surface the assumptions underlying both your position and the steelman
