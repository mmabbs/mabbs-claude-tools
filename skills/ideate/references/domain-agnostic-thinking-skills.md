## Domain-Agnostic Thinking Skills

Sixteen cognitive operations, stripped of domain-specific framing to apply universally. Operations 1–8 are adapted from the MonadFramework; operations 9–16 are original extensions built in the same spirit. Each is a structured way to approach a specific type of problem.

---

### 1. Forced Recombination ("Collision-Zone Thinking")

**Core operation:** Systematically collide unrelated domains through "what if X was Y" framing to generate novel possibilities.

**Why it works:** Creativity research shows breakthrough ideas rarely emerge from incremental improvement within a domain—they come from importing structures from elsewhere. This skill externalizes that process.

**Applications:**
- **Problem-solving:** "What if this supply chain problem was a cardiovascular system?"
- **Product design:** "What if this software interface was a conversation?"
- **Strategy:** "What if entering this market was like terraforming?"
- **Learning:** "What if this math concept was a dance?"

**Process:**
1) Name the stuck domain
2) Select a wildly unrelated domain
3) Force combination: "What if A was B?"
4) Map where the metaphor holds
5) Map where it breaks (often equally valuable)
6) Extract transferable structures

### 2. State-Task Matching ("Cognitive Variability")

**Core operation:** Recognize four cognitive modes (2×2 matrix of zoom level × exploration/connection) and deliberately match them to task demands.

| State | Mode | Best For |
|-------|------|----------|
| **Dispersed** | Zoomed in + Exploring | Generating options, brainstorming |
| **Diversified** | Zoomed out + Exploring | Synthesis, seeing patterns, strategy |
| **Focused** | Zoomed out + Connecting | Refinement, quality control |
| **Biased** | Zoomed in + Connecting | Execution when direction is clear |

**Key insight:** All states exhaust when overstayed. Recovery comes from *movement*, not rest.

**Applications:**
- **Meetings:** Dispersed for ideation → Diversified for prioritization → Focused for decision → Biased for action items
- **Learning:** Dispersed exploration → Diversified connection-making → Focused drilling → Biased application
- **Debugging:** Biased when you have a hypothesis, Dispersed when you don't, Diversified when symptoms don't cluster
- **Self-diagnosis:** "I'm stuck" often means "I'm in the wrong state for this task"

### 3. Multi-Lens Synthesis ("Perspective Aggregation")

**Core operation:** Combine multiple viewpoints into unified understanding while *preserving* divergence rather than resolving it prematurely.

**Why it works:** Traditional synthesis flattens disagreement into consensus. This preserves the information content of disagreement—where perspectives diverge tells you something important.

**Process:**
1) Gather multiple perspectives
2) Map common elements (what everyone agrees on)
3) Map divergence points (where and why they disagree)
4) Build consensus at meta-level (what the pattern of agreement/disagreement reveals)
5) Quantify remaining uncertainty

**Applications:**
- **Decision-making:** Five advisors give five different recommendations → the pattern of disagreement reveals the actual decision factors
- **User research:** Contradictory feedback → negative space between perspectives reveals the real issue
- **Team conflict:** Don't pick a winner; map what each position sees that others miss
- **Self-reflection:** Your past selves had different beliefs—aggregate rather than dismiss

### 4. Assumption Archaeology ("Critical Perspective")

**Core operation:** Structured questioning using three techniques: assumption surfacing, perspective shifting, and gap identification.

**The three questions:**
1) **Assumption surfacing:** "What am I taking for granted here?"
2) **Perspective shifting:** "How would [stakeholder X] see this differently?"
3) **Gap identification:** "What's missing that will become obvious later?"

**State-based priority:**
- Apply hardest in **Biased** state (tunnel vision risk highest)
- Apply moderately in **Focused** state (framework blindness)
- Apply lightly in **Dispersed** state (prune irrelevant threads)
- Apply sparingly in **Diversified** state (avoid analysis paralysis)

**Applications:**
- **Planning:** What am I assuming won't change?
- **Hiring:** What do I assume "good candidate" means?
- **Product:** What convenience does my solution rely on?
- **Relationships:** What am I assuming the other person wants?

### 5. Invariant Extraction ("Pattern Synthesis")

**Core operation:** Identify what *persists* across variations—the signal that survives noise.

**Five functions:**
1) **Invariant Detection** — what stays constant across iterations
2) **Structure Extraction** — recognizing repeated forms
3) **Noise Filtering** — distinguishing meaningful signals from irrelevant data
4) **Meta-Pattern Recognition** — patterns within patterns
5) **Generalization** — moving from specific to universal

**Why it works:** What survives multiple passes is structurally important. Evolution works this way—beneficial traits persist; neutral traits drift; harmful traits disappear.

**Applications:**
- **Learning from failure:** What recurs across your mistakes? That's the root cause.
- **Skill development:** What do you always do before your best performances? That's your warm-up ritual.
- **Relationship patterns:** What dynamics recur across your relationships? That's your attachment style.
- **Project post-mortems:** What problems reappear across projects? That's organizational DNA.

### 6. Bounded Exploration ("Divergence Control")

**Core operation:** Manage tension between staying on-track and productive wandering by explicitly calibrating divergence levels.

**The sweet spot:** 30-50% divergence for effective exploration

**Key mechanisms:**
1) **Anchor** to a core problem/goal (the non-negotiable)
2) **Measure** how far current work diverges from anchor
3) **Establish** coherence boundaries ("wild but not that wild")
4) **Conduct** regular alignment checks
5) **Deliberately introduce** unexplored directions when too converged

**Applications:**
- **Research:** Define what's "in scope" explicitly so you know when you're wandering productively vs. procrastinating
- **Meetings:** "Let's let this diverge for 10 minutes, then re-anchor"
- **Innovation:** Create designated "wild zones" where anything goes, separate from core work
- **Collaboration:** Agree on immovable anchors while allowing maximum divergence elsewhere

### 7. Symbolic Scaffolding ("Supreme Mathematics")

**Core operation:** Map concepts to a progression structure that provides meaning, sequence, and completion signals.

**The pattern:** A numbered or staged framework where each position has semantic weight:
- Position implies sequence (what comes before/after)
- Position implies relationship (how elements relate)
- Position implies completeness (when the cycle closes)

**Why it works:** Humans naturally chunk information into meaningful sequences. Explicit scaffolding externalizes that, making implicit structure discussable.

**Applications:**
- **Project phases:** Define stages with names that carry meaning beyond "Phase 1, 2, 3"
- **Learning progressions:** Map skill development to stages with qualitative character
- **Rituals and habits:** Daily/weekly/monthly practices mapped to different functions
- **Decision frameworks:** Numbered criteria where order implies priority

**Note:** The specific system (Supreme Mathematics, DIKW pyramid, Maslow's hierarchy, etc.) matters less than having *a* symbolic structure you can reason with.

### 8. Upstream Unification ("Simplification Cascades")

**Core operation:** Find the single insight that eliminates ten problems—10x improvements, not 10% improvements.

**Process:**
1) Identify duplication or repetition (same problem recurring)
2) Find the underlying abstraction (what do these have in common?)
3) Unify into single, more powerful intervention
4) Watch complexity cascade away downstream

**Why it works:** Most complexity is downstream of a few root causes. Fixing downstream symptoms is whack-a-mole; fixing upstream causes is leverage.

**Applications:**
- **Debugging:** What single misconfiguration explains five symptoms?
- **Management:** What one unclear expectation causes three types of conflict?
- **Personal productivity:** What one habit change would make five other changes automatic?
- **System design:** What single abstraction would eliminate three redundant components?

**The test:** If solving one problem would make other problems disappear or become trivial, you've found an upstream unification.

### 9. Inversion ("Pre-mortem Thinking")

**Core operation:** Flip the question. Instead of asking how to succeed, ask how to guarantee failure—then avoid those things.

**Why it works:** We're better at spotting danger than imagining success, and inversion exploits that asymmetry. Avoiding stupidity is more reliable than seeking brilliance. As Charlie Munger said: "Invert, always invert."

**Process:**
1) State your goal
2) Invert the question: "How could I guarantee this fails?"
3) List failure modes—specific, vivid, honest
4) Flip each failure into a safeguard
5) Check whether your plan addresses the top failure modes

**Pre-mortem variant:** Assume the project has already failed—it's six months from now and it's dead. Write the post-mortem, rank the causes, prevent the top killers now.

**Applications:**
- **Product launch:** How could we guarantee nobody uses this?
- **Hiring:** How could we hire the worst possible person?
- **Habit change:** How could I guarantee I quit within a week?
- **Strategy:** How could a competitor destroy us?

**Note:** Works best when you're feeling confident—that's when blind spots are widest.

### 10. First Principles ("Axiomatic Thinking")

**Core operation:** Decompose a problem to its fundamental truths—things true regardless of convention—then rebuild the solution from scratch using only those truths.

**Why it works:** Most thinking is reasoning by analogy ("this is like that, so do what they did"), which inherits all the assumptions of the original solution. First principles strips those away and asks: what must be true?

**Process:**
1) Name the problem
2) List current assumptions—how is this approached, and why?
3) Strip to fundamentals—what is physically, logically, or mathematically true, independent of convention?
4) Rebuild from those truths—what would you design if nothing existed yet?
5) Compare your rebuild to the conventional solution—and understand the difference

**The decomposition test:** For each belief in your plan, ask whether it's a fundamental truth (physics, math, economics, human nature) or inherited convention. If it's convention, it's negotiable.

**Applications:**
- **Pricing:** What does this actually cost to deliver? (Ignore competitor pricing.)
- **Process:** What steps are physically necessary? (Ignore inherited workflow.)
- **Product:** What does the user fundamentally need? (Ignore the existing feature set.)
- **Career:** What do I actually need to live well? (Ignore status conventions.)

**Note:** First principles thinking is expensive—reserve it for high-stakes problems where conventional solutions feel inadequate.

### 11. Second-Order Effects ("Ripple Mapping")

**Core operation:** Trace consequence chains 2–3 steps forward. Most decisions are optimized for first-order effects; the surprises live in second- and third-order consequences.

**Why it works:** First-order thinking asks "what happens if I do X?" Second-order thinking asks "and then what? And then what after *that*?" Systems are interconnected—pulling one lever moves others. Good long-term decisions come from thinking in chains, not snapshots.

**Process:**
1) State the decision or change
2) Map first-order effects—the obvious, immediate consequences
3) For each, ask "and then what?" to reach the second-order consequences
4) Repeat once more for third-order effects (diminishing certainty, high value)
5) Flag the surprises that weren't obvious from the original decision

**Common ripple patterns:**
- **Incentive shift:** A free tier attracts users who demand support, overwhelming paid-tier service
- **Behavioral adaptation:** Speed bumps slow traffic, so drivers take residential shortcuts
- **Selection effect:** Lowering the hiring bar changes who applies, which changes culture, which changes retention

**Note:** Certainty decreases with each order—the goal is second-order *awareness*, not paralysis.

### 12. Via Negativa ("Subtractive Thinking")

**Core operation:** Instead of asking "what should I add?", ask "what should I remove?" Define the solution by elimination—the highest-leverage move is often to stop doing something, not start something new.

**Why it works:** We have an innate addition bias—asked to improve something, people overwhelmingly add rather than subtract. But removal is often more powerful: fewer dependencies, less maintenance, fewer failure modes. Nassim Taleb's "via negativa"—knowledge of what *not* to do is more robust than knowledge of what to do.

**Process:**
1) List everything currently in play—features, processes, commitments, dependencies, habits
2) For each, ask: "If this didn't exist, would I add it today?"
3) Identify removal candidates—things you'd never add if starting fresh
4) Test removal—what actually breaks? Often less than expected
5) Remove and observe—the system usually adapts

**Applications:**
- **Product:** Which features do users ignore? What if we removed them?
- **Process:** Which meetings, reports, or approvals add no value?
- **Code:** Which abstractions create more complexity than they save?
- **Life:** Which commitments drain energy without returning value?

**Note:** Removal feels like giving up—reframe it as focus. Subtraction compounds: removing one thing often makes the next removal possible.

### 13. Analogical Transfer ("Structural Mapping")

**Core operation:** Find a structurally similar solved problem in a different domain and map its solution onto your unsolved one. Not random collision—deliberate structural matching.

**Why it works:** Many problems share deep structural shapes across domains: network effects, chicken-and-egg dynamics, tragedy of the commons, principal-agent problems. Identify the *shape* of your problem and you can search for domains that already solved that shape.

**Process:**
1) Abstract your problem—strip the domain-specific details to expose the structural shape
2) Name the archetype—does it match a known pattern?
3) Search for solved instances—what domains faced this exact shape?
4) Map the solution into your domain's terms
5) Check the disanalogies—where the mapping breaks is where your risks are

**Structural archetypes:**
- **Chicken-and-egg:** need supply and demand simultaneously → marketplaces, platforms, dating apps
- **Tragedy of the commons:** individual incentives deplete a shared resource → fisheries, open source
- **Cold start:** system is useless until critical mass → social networks, languages, phone systems
- **Coordination without authority:** alignment without top-down control → open source, flocking birds

**Note:** The power is in abstracting the shape, not the surface details—and disanalogies are as valuable as analogies.

### 14. Constraint Play ("Experimental Constraints")

**Core operation:** Deliberately add or remove constraints to reveal which ones actually matter, which are self-imposed, and which are creative levers.

**Why it works:** We treat constraints as fixed when most are negotiable. Toggling them—removing real ones, adding artificial ones—reveals which are *load-bearing* (the problem changes fundamentally without them) and which are *decorative*. Artificial constraints also force creativity: "What if we had to ship tomorrow?" produces different solutions than open-ended exploration.

**Process:**
1) List all constraints—budget, time, tools, people, rules, conventions, assumptions
2) Classify each—physics (immovable), policy (negotiable), or habit (self-imposed)?
3) Remove one—"What if this didn't apply?" Does the solution change?
4) Add an artificial one—"What if we also had to ___?" Does it force a better idea?
5) Identify the load-bearing constraints and focus only on those

**Constraint experiments:**
- **Remove the budget constraint:** Is the core idea sound, just under-resourced?
- **Add a "ship tomorrow" constraint:** What's the minimum viable version?
- **Add a "no code" constraint:** Can this be solved with process instead of engineering?
- **Add an "explain to a child" constraint:** Is the concept too complex, or just poorly communicated?

**Note:** The constraint you'd most hate to remove is probably the most important one.

### 15. Temporal Shifting ("Time-Horizon Reframing")

**Core operation:** Look at the same problem from radically different time horizons to change what feels important, urgent, or obvious.

**Why it works:** We're trapped in the present tense. Short horizons make us reactive and tactical; long horizons make us strategic but disconnected from action. Jumping between time scales breaks the tyranny of the current moment. What feels urgent at one week often feels irrelevant at one year—and vice versa.

**Process:**
1) State the problem as you currently see it
2) View from 1 week—what's genuinely urgent?
3) View from 3 months—what will you wish you'd started today?
4) View from 1 year—what will still matter? What won't?
5) View from 5+ years—what trajectory does this put you on?
6) Synthesize—what shows up across horizons that you missed from one?

**The time-horizon matrix:**
- **1 week:** tactical priorities — watch for urgency bias
- **3 months:** strategic positioning, habit formation — watch for sunk-cost reasoning
- **1 year:** identity shifts, compound effects — watch for status-quo bias
- **5 years:** trajectory, regret minimization — watch for overconfident prediction

**Note:** Match the horizon to the decision's reversibility—irreversible decisions deserve longer views. The insight is often in the *contrast* between horizons, not any single one.

### 16. Steelmanning ("Strongest Opposing Case")

**Core operation:** Build the most compelling, charitable, and rigorous version of the argument you disagree with—then see if your position survives.

**Why it works:** Strawmanning (weakening the opposing argument to defeat it easily) feels good but teaches nothing. Steelmanning forces you to understand why smart people hold the opposing view. If your position survives the strongest counter-argument, it's robust. If it doesn't, you've learned something more valuable than winning.

**Process:**
1) State your current position
2) Identify the opposing view—who disagrees, and why?
3) Steelman it—build the strongest possible version, including evidence you might not have considered
4) Argue *for* the steelman—where is it genuinely right?
5) Test whether your original view survives the strongest counter-argument
6) Update—modify, strengthen, or abandon based on what you learned

**The steelman test:** It's strong enough when an advocate of that position would say "yes—maybe even better than I'd put it," and you feel a genuine moment of doubt about your own position.

**Applications:**
- **Product decision:** Why would a smart person choose the approach I rejected?
- **Feedback:** What's the most charitable interpretation of this criticism?
- **Strategy:** Why might the opposite strategy actually be smarter?
- **Self-doubt:** What's the strongest case that I *am* on the right track?

**Note:** The goal isn't to change your mind—it's to earn your confidence by stress-testing your position. But when the steelman is genuinely stronger, update.

### What Makes These Transferable

1) They're *cognitive operations*, not domain knowledge—they describe *how* to think, not *what* to think about
2) They target universal challenges — creativity, focus, integration, questioning, pattern recognition, structure, simplification, stress-testing, consequence-tracing, subtraction, structural mapping, constraint manipulation, and temporal reframing — the recurring shapes of getting unstuck, not any one domain
3) They're composable—you can use State-Task Matching to know *when* to apply Forced Recombination, or use Assumption Archaeology *on* your Upstream Unification attempts
